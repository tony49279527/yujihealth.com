## LinkedIn Article Title

Beyond “Sustainable”: Why Evidence-Ready Menstrual Products Will Define the Next Phase of Period Care

## LinkedIn Article Body

For years, sustainability has been one of the strongest narratives in the menstrual-care industry.

Reusable menstrual cups and discs can reduce dependence on disposable products. Brands are exploring new materials, more efficient packaging and longer-lasting product formats. Consumers are also asking more questions about waste, ingredients and product transparency.

But sustainability alone is no longer enough.

The next phase of the period-care industry will be shaped by a more demanding question:

**Can every important product claim be supported by clear, traceable and market-relevant evidence?**

For brand owners, distributors and sourcing teams, this changes how products should be developed—and how OEM partners should be evaluated.

## Why the conversation is changing

Several recent developments point in the same direction.

In October 2025, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration issued draft guidance addressing performance testing, labeling and premarket information for menstrual products, including tampons, pads and menstrual cups. The document remains a non-binding draft, but its direction is significant: greater consistency in testing and greater transparency in product information.

At the international level, ISO/CD 25130 is under development. Its scope covers internally and externally used menstrual products intended for both single and multiple use.

In Europe, the General Product Safety Regulation has strengthened the broader safety framework for consumer products. Meanwhile, environmental claims are facing increasing expectations for substantiation rather than vague use of terms such as “green,” “natural” or “eco-friendly.”

These developments do not create one universal compliance route. Product classification and market requirements still vary by country and product type.

However, they reveal a clear industry direction:

**Menstrual-care products are moving from a claims-led market toward an evidence-led market.**

## Evidence readiness is more than having a certificate

A certificate may be important, but evidence readiness is not a folder containing a few test reports.

It is a connected system covering five areas.

### 1. A clearly defined product and intended market

The same product cannot automatically be presented in the same way in every market.

Before testing or packaging begins, a brand should define:

- The product category and intended use
- The target country or region
- The intended user group
- Expected wear or use conditions
- Cleaning and storage requirements for reusable products
- The claims that will appear on packaging, websites and sales materials

Without this foundation, brands can spend money on tests that do not support the final product or its actual claims.

### 2. Material and ingredient transparency

Buyers increasingly need to understand what is in the product, where the materials come from and whether documentation matches the production version.

For menstrual cups and reusable discs, this may include material identity, colorants, component specifications and supplier documentation.

For pads and liners, the material structure is more complex. Topsheets, absorbent layers, backsheet films, adhesives, release papers, wrappers and printed packaging may all require separate review.

A responsible material file should therefore answer four questions:

1. What is the material or component?
2. Who supplied it?
3. Which production specification does it match?
4. What evidence supports the claims being made about it?

“Premium material” is a marketing phrase. A traceable material specification is procurement evidence.

### 3. Testing that follows the product’s real risk and claims

A long test list is not necessarily a good test strategy.

Testing should be connected to the product design, intended use, target market and claims. Depending on the product, buyers may need to consider areas such as:

- Material characterization
- Biocompatibility or irritation-related evaluation where applicable
- Contaminant assessment
- Microbiological considerations
- Absorbency or leakage performance
- Dimensional and structural consistency
- Durability over repeated use or cleaning cycles
- Packaging integrity
- Label and instruction review

The important question is not, “How many reports do you have?”

It is:

**“Can each report be connected to the exact material, design, production version and claim that will enter the market?”**

Testing without version control can create a dangerous gap between the sample that was evaluated and the product that is eventually sold.

### 4. Instructions are part of product performance

In period care, performance is not determined only by manufacturing.

Fit, insertion, removal, cleaning, changing intervals, storage and disposal can all affect the user experience. This is particularly important for menstrual cups and discs, where product education can influence comfort, leakage and repeat use.

Clear instructions should not be treated as a final packaging task.

They should be developed alongside the product and reviewed for:

- Accurate use steps
- Appropriate warnings and limitations
- Cleaning and storage guidance
- Consistent terminology
- Legibility and translation quality
- Alignment with tested use conditions

A technically sound product with unclear instructions can still become a poor market experience.

### 5. Traceability and change control

A product is not evidence-ready if the documentation becomes invalid after a material, color, supplier or packaging change.

Brands and OEM partners should agree on:

- Approved product specifications
- Approved material and component suppliers
- Sample and artwork approval records
- Batch or lot identification
- In-process and release inspection records
- Rules for notifying and approving changes
- Complaint and post-market feedback handling

This is where quality becomes operational rather than promotional.

## Reusable versus disposable is the wrong argument

The industry often frames reusable and disposable period products as opposing choices.

In reality, users have different bodies, routines, cultural contexts, water access, privacy conditions and product preferences. One person may use a menstrual cup most days and a pad as backup. Another may choose disposable products when traveling and reusable products at home.

The better industry question is not:

**“Which single format should replace all others?”**

It is:

**“How can we provide safe, understandable and well-documented choices for different users and markets?”**

Sustainability should include the full product context: manufacturing, useful life, cleaning, packaging, transport, local infrastructure and end-of-life conditions. A reusable product is not automatically the right solution for every user, and a disposable product should not escape scrutiny simply because it is familiar.

Evidence helps brands move beyond simplified claims and make more responsible product decisions.

## What buyers should ask before approving an OEM program

Before selecting a menstrual-care OEM partner, brand owners and sourcing teams should ask:

1. Can you identify the exact product specification and production version being quoted?
2. Can material and component documents be matched to that version?
3. Which tests are recommended for our target market and intended claims—and why?
4. How are samples, colors, artwork and packaging approvals recorded?
5. What production and release checks are performed?
6. How are material or supplier changes communicated?
7. Can instructions and label claims be reviewed before mass production?
8. Which documents will be available before shipment?
9. How are complaints and recurring product issues investigated?
10. Which claims should we avoid until stronger evidence is available?

These questions may make the sourcing process slightly slower at the beginning.

They also reduce expensive surprises later.

## The commercial opportunity

Evidence readiness should not be seen only as a compliance cost.

For serious period-care brands, it can become a competitive advantage.

A strong evidence system can support:

- Faster distributor and retailer reviews
- Clearer communication between product, regulatory and sourcing teams
- More consistent packaging and marketing claims
- Better control of product changes
- More efficient market expansion
- Stronger long-term relationships between brands and manufacturers

The most valuable OEM partner of the future will not simply offer a product and a price.

It will help the buyer build a reviewable product program—from the initial specification and sample to packaging, quality release and export documentation.
